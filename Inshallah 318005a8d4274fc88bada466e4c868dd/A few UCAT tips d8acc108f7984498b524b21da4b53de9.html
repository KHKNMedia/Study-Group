Verbal Reasoning

I personally break down VR into 2 categories: accuracy and speed. The most important thing to do at first is understand how to answer questions accurately i.e. get them right - no matter how fast you are, you have to click the correct response!

I think accuracy can be subdivided into 2 more categories:

  • How quickly you can find the answer
  • How well you can make inferences

You have to be able to roughly understand where the answer is in a text. For instance, perhaps this means that you‚Äôre able to locate the sentence or paragraph where the answer might be. To do this, it might be good to look at keyword scanning, as well as practicing skimming a text before answering questions and committing key ideas to memory (I‚Äôll expand on both of these a little later). At this stage, when you‚Äôre trying to train your ability to quickly find the answer in the text, don‚Äôt bother working under time pressure because you‚Äôll find it incredibly stifling. Just practice untimed to develop these skills - we can add the speed in later. But train yourself to be able to find the relevant information somewhat quickly.

If you can do that, you can now answer about 50% of questions reasonably well - but the other 50% are a bit trickier. You may have found the right information in the passage, but the answer requires you to make a further inference. This is arguably the most subjective part of VR - sometimes you think your answer is right, but it‚Äôs just... not. This skill is a lot harder to develop but it comes with practice. Make sure you look over your answers (both incorrect and correct) and read through the reasoning that Medify/Medentry provides for their answer. Oftentimes, these include the common traps and things to avoid.

In particular, be careful of T/F/CT questions! The ‚ÄúCan‚Äôt Tell‚Äù tends to throw people off. Generally speaking, ‚ÄúFalse‚Äù is a better bet if the question statement can be disproven based off information in the text, while ‚ÄúCan‚Äôt Tell‚Äù means that the question can‚Äôt be answered based off the text.

As always, practice, practice, practice.

Next comes the speed. This is where I think everybody develops their own strategies for how to quickly approach texts, and I‚Äôd recommend you try the whole gauntlet and see what works for you! This is what I did:

  • If the text is a T/F/CT, I would jump straight to the question and find the keywords in the question, scanning for them in the text.
  • If the text isn‚Äôt T/F/CT, I would first very very quickly analyse the text. I‚Äôd be aiming for a 10s analysis. All I did was summarise a sentence or set of sentences in one word or two. Sometimes, for super long texts, I would summarise paragraphs in a word. For instance, in my ‚Äúanalysis‚Äù on a text about the history of the light bulb, I might mark certain sentences/paragraphs as ‚Äúinvention, production, expansion, AC/DC, conclusion‚Äù. By doing this, I was able to keep a rough mental model of the structure of the text. Then, for each question, I already knew roughly where the relevant information was and could then keyword scan for the right words to answer the question.

Decision Making

Trust yourself. Trust the process.

Practice syllogisms, writing down logical statements with a marker (no need for time pressure yet!) until you feel comfortable thinking about things in your head. The hardest part is not second-guessing yourself; it‚Äôs so easy to not trust the process you‚Äôve taken to arrive at the answer.

Logic puzzles: use a marker to illustrate relationships. With this, just... practice.

I don‚Äôt think there‚Äôs a magic bullet to DM: it‚Äôs all practice.
